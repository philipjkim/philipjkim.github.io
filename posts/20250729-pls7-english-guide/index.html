Game Guide for Sampyong Hi-Lo (a.k.a. PLS7: Pot Limit Sampyong - 7 or better)

2025-07-29philipjkim
#poker  #holdem  #PLS7  #game  #guide 

Sampyong Hi-Lo (a.k.a. PLS7: Pot Limit Sampyong - 7 or better) is a new poker variant based on Pot-Limit Omaha Hi-Lo (PLO8), with modified rules to allow for a wider variety and more powerful hands to occur frequently.

1. Core Rules#

  • Base Game: Pot-Limit Omaha Hi-Lo. Betting is limited to the pot size, and the pot is split between the highest (High) and lowest (Low) hands.
  • Hand Formation (Most Important Change):
    • Each player receives 3 cards instead of 4. Five community cards are dealt on the board. (Total 8-card pool)
    • Unlike Omaha’s ‘2 hole cards + 3 board cards’ rule, in Sampyong Hi-Lo, players can freely use any number (0-3) of their hole cards from the total 8-card pool to make the best 5-card (exception: 6-card for Three Pair) High hand and Low hand.
  • Low Hand Rule: Adheres to the strengthened ‘7-or-better’ rule, which is stricter than standard Hi-Lo games. This means a Low hand must consist of five different cards ranked 7 or below to qualify. (The best possible Low hand is A, 2, 3, 4, 5)
  • Recommendation for 4 players or fewer: For 4 or fewer players, it is recommended to play Sampyong (PLS) focusing only on High hands, excluding the Low rule. In 2-3 player games, frequent Hi/Lo pot splits can make the game slow.

2. Additional Special Hands#

These new special hands add more excitement to Sampyong Hi-Lo.

Skip Straight#

  • Condition: A 5-card combination where numbers skip one rank consecutively. (e.g., 2, 4, 6, 8, T)
  • Role of Ace: Can be used as 1 for the lowest straight (A, 3, 5, 7, 9) or as 14 for the highest straight (6, 8, T, Q, A).
  • Possible Combinations (stronger as you go down)
    • A, 3, 5, 7, 9
    • 2, 4, 6, 8, T
    • 3, 5, 7, 9, J
    • 4, 6, 8, T, Q
    • 5, 7, 9, J, K
    • 6, 8, T, Q, A

Three Pair#

  • Condition: A case where three pairs are formed using 6 cards from the total 8-card pool. This is the only hand that requires 6 cards to be completed; all other hands are completed with 5 cards.
  • Example: If your hand is 3, 4, J and the board is A, 3, 4, 7, J, three pairs (3s, 4s, Js) are formed.

3. Hand Rankings & Probabilities#

The final ranking, including all special hands, was determined by recalculating the probability of each hand appearing within an 8-card pool (your 3 hole cards + 5 board cards).

NOTE: There is a crucial change in hand ranking compared to existing major poker games (Hold’em, Omaha, Seven-Card Stud). In Sampyong Hi-Lo, Three of a Kind (Trips) ranks higher than a Straight. The reason for this will be explained in detail in the Appendix.

4. How to Play#

  • Blinds: Small Blind (SB) and Big Blind (BB) place mandatory bets.
  • Pre-flop: Each player receives 3 hole cards, and the first betting round begins.
  • Flop, Turn, River: Community cards are dealt in sequence: 3 on the Flop, 1 on the Turn, and 1 on the River. A betting round occurs after each deal.
  • Showdown: After the final betting round, remaining players reveal their hands.
  • Pot Distribution: The player with the best High hand takes half of the High Pot, and the player with the best Low hand takes half of the Low Pot. (If no player qualifies for a Low hand, the High hand takes the entire pot.)

Enjoy ‘Sampyong Hi-Lo’ with its new rules and hands, making the game even more exciting!

Probabilities are determined by the total number of cards available at each stage:

  • Turn: Your 3 hole cards + 4 board cards = Total 7 cards for combination

  • Decrease in One Pair / Two Pair Probability: While One Pair or Two Pair have a very high probability on the Flop, as more cards are added on the Turn and River, hands often develop into higher ranks (e.g., Three of a Kind, Straight), making their relative probabilities appear to decrease.
  • Decrease in High Card Probability: As more cards become available, the probability of forming any combination increases, so the likelihood of ending with just a High Card (no hand made) decreases sharply.

Why Three of a Kind ranks higher than Straight in Sampyong Hi-Lo#

Key Premise: Exclusive Probability of the ‘Final Hand’#

The first thing to remember is that the probabilities we are discussing refer to the ‘probability that the highest hand finally completed is exactly that hand.’

  • Example: If an 8-card pool contains A♠ A♥ A♦ K♣ K♥ Q♦ J♣ 2♠, the final hand for this pool is a ‘Full House’. Although this hand satisfies the ‘A Triple’ condition, it is not categorized as ‘Three of a Kind’ because a higher hand, ‘Full House’, was formed.

This principle is the key to understanding the probability reversal.

Step 1: Analyzing the Turn Situation (Total 7-card pool)#

On the Turn, a total of 7 cards are used. At this stage, Three of a Kind is more commonly determined as the final hand than a Straight.

  • Three of a Kind (Final Hand Probability: ~4.0%):

    • Forming a Three of a Kind with 7 cards is relatively easy. Many hands end up with a Three of a Kind (e.g., A-A-A-K-Q-J-2) without upgrading to a Full House.
    • Example: When 7 cards are drawn randomly, the number of combinations where the final hand remains ‘Three of a Kind’ (like A-A-A-K-Q-J-2) is greater than the number of ‘Straight’ combinations, as explained below.

  • Straight (Final Hand Probability: ~4.0%):

    • Forming a Straight with 7 cards is slightly trickier than a Three of a Kind. Five cards must be in sequence, and it must not also form a higher hand like a Flush or Full House.
    • Example: A hand like A-K-Q-J-T-3-2 where a Straight is completed but not a Straight Flush.

▶ Turn Conclusion: With 7 cards, it is more common for the final hand to remain ‘Three of a Kind’ without upgrading to another higher hand, compared to remaining ‘Straight’.

Step 2: Analyzing the River Situation (Total 8-card pool) - The Moment of Grand Reversal#

Now, with the addition of the 8th card, the fate of the two hand types dramatically diverges.

The Fate of Three of a Kind: Massive Outflow to ‘Full House’#

Numerous hands that were ‘Three of a Kind’ at the 7-card stage are massively ‘promoted’ to the more powerful ‘Full House’ when they receive the 8th card.

  • Scenario: Let’s assume your 7-card pool up to the Turn was A♠ A♥ A♦ K♣ Q♦ J♣ 2♠.

    • Current Final Hand: Three of a Kind (Aces)

  • 8th Card Appears: What happens if the River card is one of K, Q, J, or 2?

    • Example: A K♥ is dealt on the River.
    • Your 8-card pool: A♠ A♥ A♦ K♣ K♥ Q♦ J♣ 2♠
    • New Final Hand: A A-A-A-K-K combination becomes possible, making your final hand a ‘Full House’ instead of ‘Three of a Kind’.

  • Why this happens frequently: If you completed a Three of a Kind at the 7-card stage, the remaining 4 cards are all different ranks. The probability of the 8th card pairing with one of these 4 ranks is quite high, meaning upgrades from Three of a Kind to Full House occur very frequently.

  • Result: Due to this ‘massive outflow’ phenomenon, the proportion of hands where the ‘final hand remains Three of a Kind’ when all 8 cards are revealed is significantly reduced compared to the 7-card stage.

The Fate of Straight: Steady Inflow and Low Outflow (Inflow > Outflow)#

A Straight experiences the exact opposite phenomenon to a Three of a Kind.

  • Steady Inflow: There are many hands that are in a ‘draw’ state for a Straight, just barely incomplete, at the 7-card stage. The 8th card fills these missing ’links’, giving rise to numerous new Straights.

    • Example: A hand like A-K-Q-J-9-5-4 at the Turn that becomes a Straight when a T falls on the River.

  • Very Low Outflow: Once a Straight is formed, the only way it can be upgraded to a higher hand by the 8th card is to become a ‘Straight Flush’. This is a statistically very rare event. Therefore, most newly formed Straights remain as ‘Straight’ as their final hand.

Summary#

Hand Primary Role of 8th Card Result
Straight Fills missing links to ‘complete’ the hand Increased inflow into the Straight category
Three of a Kind Pairs with an existing Three of a Kind to ‘upgrade’ it Massive outflow into the Full House category

Thus, in ‘Sampyong Hi-Lo’ which uses 8 cards, the ‘inflow’ to Straights is much greater than the ‘outflow’ from Three of a Kind to Full House, resulting in a probability reversal where Straight becomes a more common hand than Three of a Kind in the final rankings.
